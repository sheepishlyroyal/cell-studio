STEP 01

REPLICATION

Before a cell divides, every strand of DNA is unzipped by helicase and copied — letter for letter — into two identical helices by DNA polymerase. Errors are under 1 in 10 million.

STEP 02

TRANSCRIPTION

RNA polymerase binds to a promoter, opens the helix, and transcribes one strand into messenger RNA at about 40 nucleotides per second.

STEP 03

TRANSPORT

After splicing, capping, and polyadenylation, the mRNA leaves the nucleus through a nuclear pore and travels to a ribosome in the cytoplasm.

STEP 04

TRANSLATION

The ribosome reads the mRNA in groups of three — codons — and links amino acids into a polypeptide that folds into a finished protein.

01 · THE CENTRAL DOGMA

Information flows from DNA to RNA to Protein.

First proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, the central dogma is the master blueprint every living cell follows. DNA is the archive — a stable, two-strand library of instructions. RNA is the messenger — a disposable, single-strand copy. Protein is the machine — the shape-shifter that does almost every job in the cell.

Each step is catalyzed by specialized enzymes. Each step has quality control. Each step can fail — and when it does, the cell has repair systems to clean up the mess. In this game, you operate every stage of the pipeline yourself.

Fun fact: A single cell does enough transcription every minute to build a library equal to the works of Shakespeare.

02 · LEVEL 1 — DNA BUILDING

Two strands. One zipper of hydrogen bonds.

DNA is a double helix. Each rung of the ladder is a pair of bases held together by hydrogen bonds: A pairs with T using two hydrogen bonds, and C pairs with G using three. That extra bond makes C-G pairs stronger — regions of DNA rich in C and G are harder to unzip.

The two strands run antiparallel: one goes 5' to 3', the other 3' to 5'. The sugar-phosphate backbone wraps around the outside while the bases face inward, hidden from water. One full twist of the helix is about 10.5 base pairs.

In Level 1, a template strand appears on the left. Your job: place the complementary base on the right. Three mutations and the strand collapses — your cell takes damage.

03 · LEVEL 2 — TRANSCRIPTION

Copy the code into a portable message.

PROMOTER

Click anywhere in the track when the droplet is over the promoter.

DNA never leaves the nucleus — it's too precious to risk. Instead, an enzyme called RNA polymerase binds a sequence called the promoter, unzips the double helix, and reads one strand letter by letter, building a complementary strand of messenger RNA.

RNA differs from DNA in three ways: it is single-stranded, it uses the sugar ribose instead of deoxyribose, and it uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T). Otherwise the pairing rules are the same — A to U, C to G.

In eukaryotes the raw transcript (pre-mRNA) is then modified: a 5' cap is added for stability, introns are spliced out, and a poly-A tail of 200+ adenines is attached to the 3' end. Only then is the mRNA export-ready.

In Level 2, the polymerase droplet bounces across the screen. Land it on the promoter three times to finish transcription. Miss too often and the cell shuts transcription down.

04 · LEVEL 3 — mRNA MAZE

A dangerous journey through the cytoplasm.

Once mRNA is made, it has to travel from the nucleus, out through a nuclear pore complex (each cell has thousands of these gates), and across the cytoplasm to a ribosome. Along the way it faces enemies — enzymes called RNases that shred unprotected RNA — and it needs ATP energy to keep moving.

The 5' cap and poly-A tail aren't just decoration. They physically protect the ends of the message from enzymes, buying enough time to be translated. Typical mRNA lifespan in a human cell is minutes to hours.

In Level 3 you steer the mRNA through a maze. Dodge enzyme hazards, collect ATP energy packets, and reach the ribosome before your timer — or your cell health — runs out.

Purple = mRNA · Red = enzyme · Yellow = energy · Green = ribosome

05 · LEVEL 4 — TRANSLATION

Read the code, three letters at a time.

The ribosome reads mRNA in groups of three nucleotides — codons. Each codon specifies one amino acid. With 4 letters in 3 positions, there are 64 possible codons to code for 20 amino acids plus start and stop signals. AUG is the universal start codon (also codes for methionine). UAA, UAG, UGA are stop codons — they terminate translation.

06 · WHEN THINGS GO WRONG

Every mistake has a biological cost.

All four levels feed into one shared Cell Health bar. Each mistake type maps to a real biological failure mode:

POINT MUTATION

One base swapped for another. If a purine swaps for a pyrimidine, the mutation is a transversion; otherwise a transition. Most are silent, but some change the amino acid and break the protein.

MISSENSE

A codon change produces a different amino acid. Sometimes harmless, sometimes devastating — sickle cell anemia is a single A→T substitution in hemoglobin.

NONSENSE

A codon changes into a premature stop codon. Translation halts early, producing a truncated, usually nonfunctional protein.

FRAMESHIFT

An insertion or deletion shifts the reading frame. Every codon downstream is rewritten — usually catastrophic.

FAILED TRANSCRIPTION

RNA polymerase binds the wrong promoter or produces a faulty transcript. The cell wastes resources and may enter stress response.

mRNA DEGRADATION

RNases chew up unprotected message before it reaches a ribosome. No mRNA, no protein, no function.

MISFOLDED PROTEIN

Wrong amino acid sequence, wrong 3D shape, wrong job. Some misfolded proteins (prions) even corrupt neighbors.

CELL DEATH

If errors pile up faster than repair enzymes can fix them, the cell triggers apoptosis — programmed self-destruction.

07 · THE CELL HEALTH BAR

One unified stakes system across all four levels.

CELL HEALTH: 100%

Mutations in Level 1, missed promoters in Level 2, enzyme hits in Level 3, and wrong amino acids in Level 4 all drain the same cell-health bar. Hit zero and the cell dies — game over, dissolve effect, death melody. Recover health by reaching new levels or collecting ATP energy in the maze.
